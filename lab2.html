Lab 2 — India Air Quality & Crop Yield: Exploratory Data Analysis¶

Datasets:

  • city_day.csv — Daily AQI readings for 26 Indian cities, January 2015 – July 2020 (29,531 rows)
  • crop_production.csv — District-level crop production across 33 Indian states, 1997–2015 (246,091 rows)

Setup: Imports and Preprocessing¶

All preprocessing from Lab 1 is re-applied.
Steps performed:

  1. Load both raw CSVs using relative paths.
  2. Parse the Date column and extract Year and Month features.
  3. Map each city to its corresponding state using the authoritative city→state dictionary.
  4. Drop rows where AQI is missing (they cannot contribute to trend or merge analyses).
  5. Impute remaining numeric pollutant columns with their column medians.
  6. Standardise state names in both datasets with str.strip().str.title() to ensure consistent join keys.

Task 6 — AQI Trend Over Time (2 marks)¶

Analytical Question¶

Has India's air quality been getting better or worse between 2015 and 2020?

Approach¶

We group the cleaned AQI dataset by Year and compute the mean AQI across all 26 monitoring cities.
We then plot the result as a line chart and annotate the year of best and worst air quality with distinct coloured markers.
A lower AQI value means cleaner air; a higher value means more pollution.

Observations — Is air quality getting better or worse?¶

Good news: India's air has been getting steadily cleaner every year since 2015.
In 2015, the average pollution score across 26 cities sat at around 212 — a level classified as "Poor" on India's national index.
By the first half of 2020, that number had dropped to roughly 114, which sits closer to the "Moderate" zone.
That is a fall of nearly 100 points — almost a halving of average pollution — in just five years.

The sharpest single-year drop came between 2018 and 2019, when mean AQI fell from 183 to 157.
The 2020 figure (114) should be read with caution: it covers only January through June, which are generally cleaner months anyway. A full-year 2020 average would almost certainly be higher.

Still, the overall direction is encouraging. Whether this reflects government policy, industry slowdowns, changing weather, or a combination of all three is beyond what this dataset alone can tell us — but the downward trend is clear and consistent.

Task 7 — Seasonal AQI Pattern (2 marks)¶

Analytical Question¶

An NGO has claimed that AQI is worst during October–December, mainly because farmers in north India burn leftover crop residue after the rice harvest. Does the data support this claim?

Approach¶

We group all valid AQI readings by Month (1–12) and compute the mean AQI for each month, pooling all years and all cities.
A bar chart makes the seasonal pattern easy to read. We add a horizontal reference line at the overall dataset mean so it is clear which months are above- and below-average.
Month names replace numeric codes on the x-axis for clarity.

Observations — Does the data support the NGO's Oct–Dec claim?¶

The NGO's claim is broadly correct, but the peak is slightly earlier than October–December.

Looking at the chart:

  • November is the single worst month (mean AQI ≈ 242), followed closely by January (≈ 232) and December (≈ 227).
  • October (≈ 189) is also well above the overall mean of 166, confirming that air quality deteriorates from October onward.
  • The cleanest months are July and August (AQI ≈ 112–114), which correspond to the monsoon season when rainfall washes particulates out of the atmosphere.
  • Air quality begins to worsen from September onwards as monsoon rains withdraw, paddy harvests conclude, and farmers burn stubble across Punjab, Haryana, and Uttar Pradesh.

Conclusion: The NGO's claim is supported by the data. The Oct–Dec window is genuinely worse than the annual average. However, November is the true peak month, and January is almost as bad — suggesting that winter temperature inversions trapping cold, stagnant air are an equally important driver alongside crop burning. The NGO is right to raise the alarm, but crop residue burning is likely one factor among several.

Task 8 — Merge Datasets & Correlation Analysis (3 marks)¶

Analytical Question¶

Is there any measurable relationship between a state's air pollution level and its agricultural output?

Why the transformation is needed¶

The two datasets operate at very different granularities:

Dataset Grain Geography Time
city_day.csv One row per city per day 26 cities (no state column) 2015–2020
crop_production.csv One row per district per season per crop 33 states via district 1997–2015

To join these tables we must:

  1. Lift AQI data from city-day to state-level by adding a State column via the city→state map.
  2. Aggregate crop data from district-season-crop to state-level by summing Area and Production.
  3. Use a common key: State (after standardising naming conventions in both datasets).

Observations — Two Most Interesting Relationships¶

1. AQI vs Total Production: r ≈ −0.19 (weak negative)
There is a slight tendency for states with higher mean AQI to have lower total crop production. This is counter-intuitive at first glance — one might expect India's major agricultural states (Uttar Pradesh, Punjab) to show both high production and high pollution. In practice, Kerala, Tamil Nadu, and Karnataka — southern states with relatively clean air — produce enormous quantities of coconut, banana, and spices (high production by weight/volume), which pulls the relationship negative. The correlation is weak and almost certainly driven by crop type and state size rather than any direct pollution effect on yield.

2. AQI vs Total Area: r ≈ +0.24 (weak positive)
States that cultivate more land tend to have marginally higher AQI. The probable real-world explanation is that large agricultural states in the Indo-Gangetic Plain (Uttar Pradesh, Madhya Pradesh, Rajasthan) also host heavy industry, dense road traffic, and biomass burning — all coincidental drivers of air pollution that come bundled with geographic scale. Farmland area alone does not cause pollution; rather, both are proxies for population density and industrial activity.

Bottom line: Both correlations are numerically small and likely reflect confounding variables. Neither supports a strong direct link between air pollution and agricultural productivity in this dataset.

Honourable Minister,

We write to share three findings from our analysis of air quality readings across 26 Indian cities and crop production records spanning 33 states.

Finding 1 — Air quality is improving. The average pollution score across all monitored cities fell from 212 in 2015 to 114 in the first half of 2020 — a drop of nearly 100 points over five years. This is a meaningful and consistent improvement.

Finding 2 — Winter months are the most dangerous. November (average score 242) and January (232) are the worst months of the year, both well above the annual average of 166. Finding 3 — The most polluted states are Gujarat, Bihar, Haryana, Delhi, and Uttar Pradesh. States in the Indo-Gangetic Plain carry by far the heaviest pollution burden. In contrast, Mizoram, Meghalaya, and Kerala record the cleanest air.

Honest limitation: While this analysis shows that high-pollution states tend to have somewhat lower crop output, we cannot conclude that pollution is causing lower yields. The relationship is weak (correlation coefficient: −0.19) and is almost certainly shaped by confounding factors such as rainfall, irrigation access, soil quality, and crop type — none of which are captured in this dataset. Policy decisions should not rest on this correlation alone.

We remain available to discuss these findings at your convenience.
